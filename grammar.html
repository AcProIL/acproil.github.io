Original flyer in Interlingua

IL. (or Latino sine Flexione) is an international auxiliary language resulting from the work of the Academia pro Interlingua. In Interlingua, any term is international in the form of the Latin theme (root) without grammar. The one who writes in IL. removes any unnecessary grammar element.

From there, with the simple help of a national language-Latin dictionary (distributed in any nation), you understand the few words of IL. that you do not understand on sight.

  • Alphabet — Latin-English: a, b, c, d, e, f, g, h, i, j, k, l, m, n, o, p, q, r, s, t, u, v, w, x, y, z.
  • Orthography — Latin: æterno, habile, philosophia, theatro… If you adopt a non-Latin spelling, Latin dictionaries can no longer be consulted in many cases.
  • Stress — on the vowel preceding the last consonant, if it exists.
  • Cases — do not exist in IL.: the Latin case results from position or prepositions: de, ab, ad, in….
  • Plural — is indicated by the suffix -s: singular monte, flore; plural montes, flores.
  • Gender — The natural gender can be indicated by mas, femina; cane mas, cane femina. The grammatical gender is useless; in the same way the substantial-adjective agreement is unnecessary.
  • Articles — do not exist in IL. Ex.: Vos da ad me libro (You give me the book), illo libro (this book), meo book (my book), illo meo libro (this book of mine), uno meo libro (one of my books).
  • Intensity of adjectives — is indicated by a separate word; Ex..: breve (short), plus breve, magis breve, multo breve, ultra breve, extra breve (very short).
  • Adverb from adjective — is also sometimes indicated with separate words: Petro stude cum mente diligente (Peter studies diligently), scribe in forma elegante (writes elegantly), loque in modo claro (speaks clearly), canta forte (sings loudly), curre veloce (runs fast), dormi per longo termpore (sleeps for a long time).
  • Numeros — uno, duo, tres, quatuor… (one, two, three, four…) decem, centum, mille, millione (ten, hundred, thousand, million); decem et uno=11; sex decem=60.
  • VERB — do not have personal suffixes. me habe, te habe, illo habe, nos habe, vos habe, illos habe (I have, thou have, he/she has, we have, you have, they have).
  • Verb tense — if the sentence already indicates the time, a suffix is not necessary. Ex.: heri nos scribe, cras nos lege (yesterday we were writing, tomorrow we will read). Tense can be indicated by: heri (yesterday), jam (already), in praeterito (in the past), hodie (today), cras (tomorrow), in proximo tempore (in a near future) in futuro (in the future). Me vol scribe, me debe sctibe (I will write), me i scribe (I am going to write).
  • Verb mood —
    • The conditional is indicated with si, que, ut…
    • The infinitive has no suffix, ex:. stude, excelle, adopta…
    • The flexion -nte of the Latin present participle has a maximum internationality; it can be eliminated by the relationship: studente (student) = qui stude, mittente (sender) = qui mitte.
    • The Latin past participle, regular or irregular, lives several times in English derivatives and has a great internationality. We can eliminate it by inversion: filio es amato ab matre (the son is loved by the mother) = matre ama filio (the mother loves the son).

Publication in IL. is much preferable to publication in any national language; translation of such articles into other languages is not necessary, as the reader understands the author's original writing.
